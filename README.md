A research repository introducing OAPRM, the Open-Audience Pseudo-Psychology Risk Model, and Trust Camouflage: a framework for analyzing how viral mental-health content can arm adversarial audiences while claiming to protect victims.


# The Open-Audience Failure of Viral PseudoPsychology Content

This repository hosts the research paper and supporting framework for **OAPRM: the Open-Audience Pseudo-Psychology Risk Model**.

The core claim is simple:

When mental-health creators, therapists, platforms, and influencers publish abuse-detection rules to open audiences, victims are not the only people learning. Abusers, manipulators, false accusers, predators, and reputation managers can study the same content and use it to adapt.

The victim receives a checklist.

The abuser receives the victim’s radar.

That difference changes everything.

## Core Concepts

### OAPRM: Open-Audience Pseudo-Psychology Risk Model

OAPRM analyzes what happens when clinical, forensic, or therapeutic concepts are removed from private, case-specific contexts and distributed through public algorithms.

The model argues that viral pseudo-psychology content often assumes a protected audience of victims seeking clarity, while the actual audience is mixed.

In any open feed, at least three audience rows exist:

1. **Victims** who may finally find language for their pain.
2. **Confused observers** who may misapply labels to ordinary conflict.
3. **Adversarial viewers** who may learn which behaviors now trigger suspicion.

That third row is the failure most public mental-health content ignores.

## Trust Camouflage

**Trust Camouflage** describes what happens when a harmful person learns the public language of safety, healing, boundaries, accountability, trauma, and abuse well enough to sound safe while remaining dangerous.

The person does not necessarily become healthier.

They become harder to detect.

They learn which red flags to retire, which phrases to avoid, which masks to improve, and which therapeutic terms make them sound emotionally intelligent.

Same control.

New interface.

## Why This Matters

Clinical language requires context.

Terms like gaslighting, narcissism, love bombing, DARVO, coercive control, and red flags depend on case history, severity, repetition, power dynamics, risk assessment, and pattern verification.

When these concepts become viral soundbites, they can stop functioning as careful tools and start functioning as public scripts.

Victims may gain language.

Confused audiences may gain paranoia.

Predators may gain evasive intelligence.

## Weak Disclosure vs. Resilient Disclosure

The paper argues that much of viral mental-health content uses **weak disclosure**: public broadcasting of detection rules to mixed audiences, including adversarial viewers.

The proposed alternative is **resilient disclosure**: education designed to help victims without handing predators a manual for evasion.

Resilient disclosure may include:

* context gates
* severity scaffolding
* pattern verification
* audience-risk checks
* adversarial-risk scoring
* separation between public awareness and case-specific guidance
